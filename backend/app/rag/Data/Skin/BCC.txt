Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common skin cancer, a slow-growing malignancy from basal cells in the epidermis, rarely metastasizing but locally destructive if untreated.
Definition
Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most prevalent non-melanoma skin cancer, originating from basal keratinocytes in the basal layer of the epidermis or hair follicles, forming locally invasive pearly nodules or plaques. It rarely metastasizes (<0.1%) but aggressively erodes surrounding tissue, with subtypes including nodular (most common, 60-80%), superficial, morpheaform (sclerosing/infiltrative), and pigmented; UV-induced mutations in PTCH1/SMO (Hedgehog pathway) drive 90% of cases.
Symptoms
Symptoms of BCC typically include a pearly or waxy bump with rolled borders, central ulceration (rodent ulcer), telangiectasia (visible vessels), or non-healing sore that bleeds/oozes/crusts recurrently. Superficial BCC appears as scaly red patches; morpheaform as scar-like white plaques; advanced signs feature pain, numbness from perineural invasion, or eye/nose involvement. Early lesions mimic benign cysts, emphasizing biopsy for diagnosis.
Causes
The primary cause of BCC is chronic ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure from sunlight or tanning beds, inducing DNA damage in p53, PTCH1, and Hedgehog signaling genes. Ionizing radiation, arsenic, UV therapy (PUVA), and chronic skin wounds contribute; genetic syndromes like basal cell nevus (Gorlin) syndrome cause multiple BCCs via PTCH1 germline mutations, though sporadic UV exposure accounts for 95% in fair-skinned individuals.
Risk Factors
Major risk factors include fair skin (Fitzpatrick I-II), intense intermittent UV exposure (childhood sunburns), older age (>40), male sex, northern European ancestry, immunosuppression (transplant patients), and prior radiation/arsenic exposure. Genetic predispositions encompass xeroderma pigmentosum, Gorlin syndrome, and Bazex syndrome; family history and >15 lifetime sunburns double risk, with indoor tanning before age 35 increasing odds by 75%.
Diagnosis
Diagnosis involves full skin exam with dermoscopy revealing arborizing vessels, ulceration, or pigment in nodular BCC, confirmed by shave/incisional/excisional biopsy showing peripheral palisading basaloid cells and retraction artifact. Mohs micrographic surgery provides same-day margin control; imaging (CT/MRI) assesses deep invasion; subtypes guide therapy, with immunohistochemistry (BerEP4+) distinguishing from squamous cell carcinoma.
Treatment
Treatment prioritizes complete excision: Mohs micrographic surgery (gold standard for high-risk face/eyes, 99% cure), standard excision (4mm margins for low-risk), curettage/electrodesiccation for superficial/small lesions, or cryotherapy/topical 5-FU/imiquimod for select superficial BCC. Radiation suits inoperable elderly patients; Hedgehog inhibitors (vismodegib, sonidegib) for advanced/metastatic/locally advanced cases; recurrence monitoring every 6-12 months essential.
Prevention
Prevention emphasizes sun protection: broad-spectrum SPF 30+ reapplied every 2 hours, UPF clothing/hats, shade-seeking 10am-4pm, and avoiding tanning beds entirely. Annual dermatologist skin checks for high-risk individuals, self-exams monthly, and early treatment of actinic keratoses reduce incidence; public campaigns highlight "Slip! Slop! Slap! Wrap!" as 90% of BCC links to modifiable UV exposure.
