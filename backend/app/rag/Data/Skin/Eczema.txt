Eczema, also known as atopic dermatitis, is a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing itchy, red, and dry rashes, often triggered by environmental factors.
Definition
Eczema, or atopic dermatitis, is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin disorder characterized by intense itching (pruritus), dry skin, and red, inflamed patches that may ooze, crust, or thicken over time. It typically affects the flexural areas like elbows and knees in children but can appear anywhere in adults, driven by a defective skin barrier (filaggrin gene mutations) and immune dysregulation leading to Th2-skewed responses. The condition varies in severity, with flares alternating between remission periods.
Symptoms
Symptoms of eczema include severe itching (often worse at night), red or brownish-gray inflamed patches, small raised bumps that leak fluid and crust, thickened or scaly skin from scratching, and dry/sensitive skin prone to cracking. Acute flares feature weeping blisters, while chronic cases show lichenification (leathery texture); associated signs include sleep disturbance, secondary infections, and eye irritation like conjunctivitis.
Causes
Eczema arises from a combination of genetic predisposition (impaired skin barrier function via filaggrin defects), immune system overactivity (elevated IgE and Th2 cytokines like IL-4/13), and environmental triggers that disrupt the skin microbiome. Key triggers include allergens (dust mites, pollen, pet dander), irritants (soaps, fabrics), stress, food sensitivities (in infants), microbial infections (Staphylococcus aureus), and climate factors like low humidity or temperature changes.
Risk Factors
Major risk factors for eczema include family history of atopy (asthma, hay fever, eczema), early-life exposure to allergens or pollutants, urban living, and genetic mutations like FLG loss-of-function. Other factors encompass breastfeeding avoidance in infancy, antibiotic overuse disrupting gut flora, obesity, stress, and racial/ethnic predispositions (higher in African/Asian descent); severe cases often link to filaggrin deficiency and high IgE levels.
Diagnosis
Diagnosis of eczema is primarily clinical, based on history and exam using criteria like the UK Working Party (itch plus 3+ features: flexural rash, dry skin, visible dermatitis, onset <2 years). Patch testing identifies contact allergens, skin biopsy (rarely) confirms spongiosis, and blood tests measure IgE/eosinophils; severity scoring via SCORAD or EASI guides management, ruling out mimics like psoriasis or scabies.
Treatment
Treatment follows a stepwise approach: emollients (fragrance-free) for barrier repair, topical corticosteroids (mild like hydrocortisone for face, potent for body) for flares, calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) for sensitive areas, and PDE4 inhibitors (crisaborole). For moderate-severe cases, systemic options include dupilumab (IL-4/13 blocker), JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib), phototherapy, or immunosuppressants (cyclosporine); wet wraps aid acute flares, with infection control via antibiotics.
Prevention
Prevention emphasizes daily moisturizing post-bathing to maintain barrier function, avoiding known triggers (harsh soaps, wool, overheating), using gentle cleansers, and early allergen avoidance in high-risk infants (e.g., exclusive breastfeeding). Bleach baths reduce Staph colonization, probiotics may help early gut microbiome, and education on "soak and smear" routines prevents flares; long-term, addressing comorbidities like asthma improves control.
