Warts are benign skin growths caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, commonly appearing on hands, feet, or genitals and often resolving spontaneously but treatable for faster clearance.
Definition
Warts are small, rough, benign skin growths caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) infection of keratinocytes, leading to epidermal hyperplasia and hyperkeratosis. Over 100 HPV types cause distinct forms: common (verruca vulgaris on hands), plantar (feet), flat (face/legs), filiform (face), and genital (condyloma acuminata); they spread via contact and may persist 1-2 years before immune clearance.
Symptoms
Symptoms include firm, rough-surfaced papules with black dots (thrombosed capillaries), often painless but tender on pressure (plantar warts); common types feature skin-colored/flesh-toned bumps 1-10mm. Flat warts appear smooth/yellowish; genital warts as soft, cauliflower-like; periungual warts cause nail distortion; most asymptomatic but may itch/bleed if traumatized.
Causes
Warts result from HPV entry through skin microtrauma, infecting basal keratinocytes and inducing proliferation via E6/E7 oncoproteins that inactivate p53/Rb. Transmission occurs directly (skin-skin) or indirectly (fomites in pools/gyms); autoinoculation spreads locally; incubation 2-6 months; not all HPV types cause warts (high-risk like HPV16 cause cancer).
Risk Factors
Key risk factors include skin trauma/cuts (hands/feet), young age (children/teens), weakened immunity (HIV, steroids), warm/moist environments (pools, saunas), crowded living, and occupations with hand exposure (meat handlers). Family clustering, atopic dermatitis, and male circumcision status influence genital warts; bites/nail-biting increase periungual risk.
Diagnosis
Diagnosis is clinical via appearance/location: rough hyperkeratotic papules with dermatoglyphic disruption (plantar), confirmed by paring (mosaic pattern) or biopsy (koilocytes, acanthosis, hypergranulosis). Acetowhitening aids genital warts; differentiate from calluses, corns, seborrheic keratosis, or molluscum; HPV typing rarely needed except anogenital.
Treatment
Treatment destroys lesions or boosts immunity: salicylic acid (17-40% daily after paring, 50-70% success), cryotherapy (liquid N2 every 2-4 weeks), cantharidin, 5-FU/imiquimod topicals, intralesional bleomycin/candida antigen, or curettage/electrosurgery. Refractory cases use pulsed-dye laser or cimetidine; observe low-risk as 65% resolve in 2 years; no HPV cure.
Prevention
Prevention involves avoiding barefoot walking in public areas, not sharing towels/razors/nail clippers, covering cuts, hand hygiene, and HPV vaccination (Gardasil9 covers types 6/11 causing 90% genital warts). Dry skin promptly, discourage nail-biting; high-risk patients use condoms; spontaneous clearance common via cell-mediated immunity.
