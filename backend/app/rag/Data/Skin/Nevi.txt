Nevi, also known as moles, are common benign skin growths formed by clusters of melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells, and most people develop 10-40 during their lifetime.
Definition
Nevi (singular: nevus) are benign proliferations of melanocytes appearing as circumscribed pigmented or skin-colored lesions on the skin, classified as congenital (present at birth) or acquired (developing later). Common types include junctional (flat, brown macules), compound (raised, dome-shaped), and intradermal (flesh-colored papules); atypical/dysplastic nevi feature irregular borders and color variation, conferring higher melanoma risk.
Symptoms
Symptoms typically involve small (1-6mm), round/oval, evenly pigmented spots that may be flat, raised, or hairy, often asymptomatic but occasionally itchy if irritated. Common nevi remain stable; dysplastic nevi show asymmetry, irregular borders, variegated color, and diameter >6mm (ABCDE changes warrant evaluation); congenital giant nevi (>20cm) carry malignant potential.
Causes
Nevi form when melanocytes cluster due to genetic factors and UV exposure triggering proliferation, with most acquired during childhood/adolescence via somatic mutations. Familial atypical multiple mole-melanoma syndrome (FAMMM) involves CDKN2A germline mutations; sun exposure accelerates nevus development, though not all nevi progress to malignancy.
Risk Factors
Key risk factors include fair skin (Fitzpatrick I-II), excessive UV exposure/sunburns, family history of melanoma/dysplastic nevi, personal history of >50 common nevi or >5 atypical nevi, and genetic syndromes like FAMMM or xeroderma pigmentosum. Childhood blistering sunburns and indoor tanning before age 30 significantly elevate atypical nevus count and melanoma odds.
Diagnosis
Diagnosis uses clinical exam with dermoscopy revealing symmetric pigment networks, dots/globules, or structureless areas in benign nevi versus atypical patterns (blue-white veil, polymorphous vessels) in dysplastic types. Biopsy (shave/excisional) confirms histology (maturation, no mitoses); total body mapping/ photography monitors high-risk patients; ABCDE rule screens for melanoma transformation.
Treatment
Treatment unnecessary for stable common nevi; cosmetic removal via shave excision, laser, or cryotherapy for irritated/prominent lesions. Dysplastic nevi excised with 2-5mm margins if suspicious; prophylactic excision debated for numerous atypical nevi in high-risk families; no systemic therapy, focus on surveillance every 3-6 months.
Prevention
Prevention emphasizes broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen daily, UPF clothing/shade avoidance 10am-4pm, no tanning beds, and monthly self-skin exams using ABCDE criteria. High-risk individuals need annual dermatologist full-body exams starting age 10-20; sun protection from infancy minimizes nevus count and dysplasia, reducing lifetime melanoma risk by 50%.
