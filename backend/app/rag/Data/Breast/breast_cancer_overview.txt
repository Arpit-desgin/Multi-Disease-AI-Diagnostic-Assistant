Breast cancer is a malignant disease where cells in the breast tissue grow uncontrollably, forming tumors that can invade nearby areas or spread to distant organs like bones, lungs, or brain.
Definition
Breast cancer comprises invasive adenocarcinomas primarily arising from ductal (IDC, 70-80%) or lobular epithelium (ILC, 10-15%), with subtypes including hormone receptor-positive (ER/PR+), HER2-enriched, triple-negative (TNBC), and rare forms like inflammatory breast cancer (IBC) or Paget's disease; noninvasive precursors like DCIS/LCIS precede invasion.
Symptoms
Common symptoms include painless lumps (upper outer quadrant), nipple retraction/discharge, skin dimpling (peau d'orange), axillary nodes, or IBC rash-like redness/swelling; advanced signs feature bone pain, jaundice, or dyspnea from metastases; early stages often asymptomatic on screening.
Causes
Sporadic cases (90%) stem from cumulative mutations (PIK3CA, TP53) driven by estrogen exposure, HER2 amplification, and microenvironment changes; hereditary (5-10%) involves BRCA1/2; risk amplified by dense breasts or prior radiation.
Risk Factors
Female sex, age >50, BRCA mutations (50-85% lifetime risk), dense breasts, nulliparity, late menopause, obesity, alcohol, HRT; protective factors include breastfeeding and oophorectomy.
Diagnosis
Triple assessment: mammogram (microcalcifications/masses), ultrasound/MRI, core biopsy (ER/PR/HER2/Ki-67); staging via TNM, sentinel node, PET-CT; Oncotype DX predicts recurrence.
Treatment
Multimodal based on stage/subtype: surgery (lumpectomy/mastectomy), radiation, chemo (taxanes), endocrine (tamoxifen/AIs), HER2-targeted (trastuzumab), CDK4/6 inhibitors, PARP (olaparib BRCA+), immunotherapy (pembrolizumab TNBC).
Prevention
Screening (mammograms 40+), risk-reducing surgery (BRCA), chemoprevention (tamoxifen high-risk), lifestyle (limit alcohol, exercise); early detection achieves 99% stage I survival.
