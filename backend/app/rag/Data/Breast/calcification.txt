Breast calcifications are calcium deposits in breast tissue visible on mammograms, often benign but sometimes signaling early breast cancer through suspicious patterns like clustered microcalcifications.
Definition
Mammogram illustrating microcalcifications within ductal carcinoma in situ. 
Breast calcifications are micro- or macro- deposits of calcium salts in breast tissue, appearing as white spots on mammography; macrocalcifications (>0.5mm) are typically benign popcorn-like or rod-shaped, while microcalcifications (<0.5mm) in tight clusters, linear/branching patterns may indicate ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive cancer due to necrotic debris from rapid cell turnover.
This mammogram shows clustered microcalcifications characteristic of DCIS, appearing as fine bright specks often requiring biopsy.
Symptoms
Breast calcifications produce no palpable symptoms or pain; detected incidentally on screening mammograms as asymptomatic white flecks/spots. Suspicious patterns prompt further imaging/biopsy, but patients notice no lumps, discharge, or skin changes unless associated with underlying malignancy.
Causes
Calcifications form from calcium precipitation in necrotic/apoptotic cells, secretory material, or fibrosis; benign causes include cysts, fibroadenomas, fat necrosis, ectatic ducts, prior surgery/radiation, or aging vessels. Malignant microcalcifications arise from DCIS tumor necrosis or invasive ductal carcinoma outgrowing blood supply, with hydroxyapatite composition differing from bone.
Risk Factors
Risk factors mirror breast cancer: age >50, dense breasts, prior biopsies showing atypia, family history, hormonal factors; benign calcifications link to fibrocystic changes, injury, or inflammation. Microcalcifications confer higher malignancy risk (12-40% biopsy yield) vs. macro (benign).
Diagnosis
Diagnosis via mammography (BI-RADS scoring: 3 probably benign, 4/5 suspicious); magnification views assess morphology/distribution (pleomorphic, linear = malignant); ultrasound differentiates solid masses; stereotactic/core biopsy samples calcifications for histology (DCIS vs. benign); tomosynthesis reduces false positives.

Treatment
Benign/macrocalcifications require no treatment, just follow-up imaging; suspicious microcalcifications trigger biopsy—negative results prompt surveillance, while DCIS/cancer leads to excision, radiation, endocrine therapy, or mastectomy based on extent/grade. No direct calcification treatment.
Prevention
Prevention focuses on breast cancer risk reduction (mammogram screening 40+, healthy weight, limit alcohol/HRT) since calcifications reflect underlying pathology; no specific calcification prevention, but early detection via annual imaging identifies 93% DCIS cases.
